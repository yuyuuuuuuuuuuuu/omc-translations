1. Introduction

This explanation serves as a commentary on the official explanation. Therefore, it does not have any particular novelty as an explanation.

2. Optimal Exchange Procedure 1

First, we note the following points.

  • If there are empty containers that can be exchanged, it is better to exchange them.
  • The order of exchanges does not affect the outcome.

Then, for example, we can think of the following exchange procedure 1.
(Assuming that all beverages are consumed immediately upon obtaining them.)

  • Drink all of AAA and exchange as many empty containers of AAA as possible for BBB.
  • Drink all of BBB and exchange as many empty containers of BBB as possible for AAA.
  • Repeat the above two steps as long as there are empty containers that can be exchanged.

From the initial points noted, this procedure becomes an optimal exchange procedure (one example).

(Let (a,b)(a,b)(a,b) denote the state where we have aaa containers of AAA and bbb containers of BBB.)

  1. From (401,401)(401,401)(401,401), exchange 399399399 empty containers of AAA to obtain 133133133 of BBB. This results in (2,534)(2,534)(2,534).
  2. From (2,534)(2,534)(2,534), exchange 532532532 empty containers of BBB to obtain 767676 of AAA. This results in (78,2)(78,2)(78,2).
  3. From (78,2)(78,2)(78,2), exchange 787878 empty containers of AAA to obtain 262626 of BBB. This results in (0,28)(0,28)(0,28).
  4. From (0,28)(0,28)(0,28), exchange 282828 empty containers of BBB to obtain 444 of AAA. This results in (4,0)(4,0)(4,0).
  5. From (4,0)(4,0)(4,0), exchange 333 empty containers of AAA to obtain 111 of BBB. This results in (1,1)(1,1)(1,1).
  6. End as there are no more exchanges possible.

This procedure itself seems quite intuitive, but there is a drawback in that, during each operation, one type of container decreases while the other increases, making it difficult to track the behavior of the number of containers.
Therefore, let's try to improve this next.

3. Optimal Exchange Procedure 2

We will consider the issues mentioned in the second section.
The problem here is that when AAA decreases, BBB increases.
Thus, we arrive at the idea that if we exchange AAA for BBB and then immediately exchange all the increased BBB back to AAA, we can achieve a change in the number of AAA without changing the number of BBB. (The exchange B→A→BB \to A \to BB→A→B can be considered similarly.)

To incorporate this idea into a solution, let's refine the details.

First, previously we were exchanging a certain amount of AAA all at once for BBB, but due to the remainder relationship, we cannot guarantee that we can exchange all the increased BBB back to AAA.
As a solution to this, we can use the fact that "the optimality of exchanges does not depend on the procedure" that we have been using.
Specifically, instead of exchanging all at once, we should exchange AAA for BBB in increments that allow all the increased BBB to be converted back to AAA, specifically 3×7=213 \times 7 = 213×7=21 at a time.

Next, there is a non-trivial issue of whether this method can be continued until the end.
However, this does not need to be a concern at this stage.
Even if some empty containers are left at the end, they will be at most 202020 or less, so it seems sufficient to apply procedure 1 afterward to handle that.

Summarizing the above, we obtain the following exchange procedure 2.

  • If there are at least 212121 unopened AAA, drink 212121 of AAA, exchange for 777 new BBB, and then drink all 777 of the exchanged BBB to exchange for 111 new AAA.
  • If there are at least 212121 unopened BBB, drink 212121 of BBB, exchange for 333 new AAA, and then drink all 333 of the exchanged AAA to exchange for 111 new BBB.
  • Repeat the above as long as at least one of the conditions can be met.
  • If there are any leftovers at the end of the repetitions, proceed as in procedure 1.

Specific Example of Exchange Procedure 2

Specifically, let's look at the behavior of the number of containers when we have 401401401 of both AAA and BBB.
(Let (a,b)(a,b)(a,b) denote the state where we have aaa containers of AAA and bbb containers of BBB.)

  1. From (401,401)(401,401)(401,401), exchange 212121 of AAA to obtain 777 of BBB. This results in (380,401+7)(380,401+7)(380,401+7). Then, exchange the obtained 777 of BBB for 111 of AAA. This results in (381,401)(381,401)(381,401).
  2. From (381,401)(381,401)(381,401), exchange 212121 of AAA to obtain 777 of BBB. This results in (360,401+7)(360,401+7)(360,401+7). Then, exchange the obtained 777 of BBB for 111 of AAA. This results in (361,401)(361,401)(361,401).
  3. Continue this process.

By looking at the number of containers before and after each step, as well as the number of beverages consumed in between, we can see that this is equivalent to the following.

  • If there are at least 212121 unopened AAA, drink 212121 of AAA, exchange for 111 new AAA, and only obtain the fact that 777 of BBB were consumed separately.
  • If there are at least 212121 unopened BBB, drink 212121 of BBB, exchange for 111 new BBB, and only obtain the fact that 333 of AAA were consumed separately.
  • Repeat the above as long as at least one of the conditions can be met.
  • If there are any leftovers at the end of the repetitions, proceed as in procedure 1.

Furthermore, by separating the parts where beverages are consumed and exchanged into the part where we obtain the fact of consumption and the part where we exchange, we can consider the following.

  • If there are at least 212121 unopened AAA, reduce unopened AAA by 202020, and only obtain the fact that 282828 beverages were consumed, consisting of 777 of BBB and 212121 of AAA separately.
  • If there are at least 212121 unopened BBB, reduce unopened BBB by 202020, and only obtain the fact that 242424 beverages were consumed, consisting of 212121 of BBB and 333 of AAA separately.
  • Repeat the above as long as at least one of the conditions can be met.
  • If there are any leftovers at the end of the repetitions, proceed as in procedure 1.

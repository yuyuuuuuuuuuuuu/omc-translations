The problem statement can be rephrased as "Determine the number of undirected graphs with NNN vertices and N+1N+1N+1 edges that do not have self-loops or multiple edges, and where each vertex has a degree of at least 111 and it is possible to repeat the movements NNN times." Therefore, first, we will confirm the following lemma.

Lemma. For any vertex, its degree is at least 222.

Proof. First, since multiple cats cannot exist in the same city, it can be seen that after the movements, there is always exactly one cat in each city. Here, let's assume there is a vertex with degree 111. If we focus on the vertex with degree 111, there would be cats both departing from and arriving at that vertex during a movement. In this case, those cats would both use the only edge that connects to the degree 111 vertex, which contradicts that cats cannot pass each other. Thus, this is shown by contradiction.

Here, since each edge connects two vertices, the sum of the degrees of all vertices is 2(N+1)=2N+22(N+1) = 2N + 22(N+1)=2N+2. Combining this with the result of the lemma, we find that the combinations of vertex degrees must be either

  • one vertex with degree 444 and N−1N-1N−1 vertices with degree 222
  • two vertices with degree 333 and N−2N-2N−2 vertices with degree 222

First, considering the former case, since all vertices except the one with degree 444 have degree 222, it follows that there are exactly 222 closed paths passing through the vertex with degree 444. We shall denote those paths as C1C_1C1​ and C2C_2C2​, and let the vertex with degree 444 be AAA. First, consider the case where a cat at AAA moves to a vertex that is part of C1C_1C1​. Then the cats at the vertices comprising C1C_1C1​ can only move one step either clockwise or counterclockwise along C1C_1C1​. However, at this time, there exists at least one vertex in C2C_2C2​ that is connected to AAA without any arriving cat, making it impossible to fulfill the condition to move appropriately. The same reasoning applies when moving to the vertices comprising C2C_2C2​, thus concluding that no valid graph exists in this case.
Next, let us consider the latter case. Let the vertices with degree 333 be designated as AAA and BBB. First, consider the case where AAA and BBB are not directly connected by an edge. In this situation, each of the 333 vertices connected to AAA must necessarily have degree 222. We will denote these 333 vertices as X,Y,ZX, Y, ZX,Y,Z. Without loss of generality, assume that the cat at AAA moves to XXX and the cat at YYY moves to AAA. Then the cat at ZZZ would have to move to a vertex other than AAA connected by an edge to ZZZ, and since there is only one such vertex, it leads to a situation where no cat arrives at ZZZ, thus failing to meet the requirements for a valid movement. Therefore, it follows that AAA and BBB must be connected directly by an edge. Furthermore, on removing the edge connecting AAA and BBB, it becomes evident that all vertices have degree 222, and consequently, all connected components become closed paths. In this case, all cats can fulfill the condition by moving one step counterclockwise along the path they belong to, indicating that the original graph also satisfies the condition. Hence, it suffices to count the number of these graphs.

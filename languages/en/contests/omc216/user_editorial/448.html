"Point QQQ is symmetric to point AAA with respect to line FGFGFG," is the same as the official explanation.
According to Brocard's theorem, in circle ABCABCABC, line FGFGFG is the polar line with respect to point EEE. Therefore, if we drop a perpendicular from EEE to FGFGFG and denote the foot of the perpendicular as HHH, points OOO, EEE, and HHH are collinear, and the relation OE×OH=302OE \times OH = 30^2OE×OH=302 holds. On the other hand, since OE2OE^2OE2 can be determined from the power of a point theorem, we can also calculate the value of EH=OH−OEEH = OH - OEEH=OH−OE at this point.
Furthermore, if we denote the intersection of line CACACA and line FGFGFG as point RRR, by the properties of a complete quadrilateral, points CCC, EEE, AAA, and RRR form a harmonic division, allowing us to determine the length of segment ARARAR.
From the above, we can now calculate the distances from line FGFGFG to points CCC, EEE, and AAA (and thus the distance to point QQQ as well). Finally, we can find the length of segment CQCQCQ by straightforwardly applying the Pythagorean theorem.

This kind of thinking, "Can we push through with the Pythagorean theorem?" may not be elegant, but it is often useful in OMC.

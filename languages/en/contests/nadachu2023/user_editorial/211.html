By coloring the squares in a checkerboard pattern with red and blue, we can reinterpret the condition of "moving to a square with a different adjacent color" to "moving to a square with the same adjacent color" by swapping the colors on the red side: if it is white, it becomes black, and if it is black, it becomes white. Therefore, we will find the total number of ways to color the squares that satisfy the latter condition.
Let us denote the coloring from the top of the column as P,Q,R,SP, Q, R, SP,Q,R,S for (black, black), (black, white), (white, black), and (white, white) respectively. When both AAA and BBB are colored black, we will calculate the total number of arrangements of P,Q,RP, Q, RP,Q,R such that QQQ and RRR are not adjacent, with either PPP or QQQ on the left and either PPP or RRR on the right, and we find that there are 494949 ways.
Similarly, when both AAA and BBB are colored white, there are also 494949 ways, so the total number of cases we are looking for is 49×2=9849 \times 2 = \textbf{98}49×2=98 ways.
